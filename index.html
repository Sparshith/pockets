Unpocketable: The Great Clothing Gender Divide

Another impulse fished in a convenient pocket for change-instantly, automatically, bringing forth a nickel for the conductor and a penny for the newsboy. These pockets came as a revelation. Of course she had known they were there, had counted them, made fun of them, mended them, even envied them; but she never had dreamed of how it felt to have pockets. Behind her newspaper she let her consciousness, that odd mingled consciousness, rove from pocket to pocket, realizing the armored assurance of having all those things at hand, instantly get-at-able, ready to meet emergencies . . .

Charlotte Perkins Gilman, "If I Were a Man (1914)

Written in 1914, Charlotte’s dream of having everyday-use things “at hand, instantly get-at-able, ready to meet emergencies” still feels like a dream today, as I write this over a century later. As a woman, most of my clothing still has no pockets, and if it rarely does, the pockets are too small to carry anything meaningful. Often, there are “fake” pockets in my clothing - patches of cloth sewed shut on clothes as ornamentation, as opposed to utility. On the other hand, men’s clothing almost always has visible, large pockets to carry their everyday belongings. (I use the gender binary here regrettably and consciously to reflect the binary nature of mainstream clothing in the market.)

Men have pockets to keep things in, women for decoration.

Christian Dior (1954) as quoted by Paul Johnson, The Spectator, 2011

What this really means is that pockets reinforce the same gender roles that we see all around us - that men do work that requires them to have utility built into their clothing, while a woman’s primary role is to look beautiful.

Now forward to the 21st Century, and women’s clothing has got some pockets, but as others have pointed out, they still won’t fit an iPhone. I thought I’d check for myself. I went around with a collection of daily-use objects of various sizes (wallet, phone, ring, power bank, card holder, earphones, keys, watch) and asked random passersby to check which / how many of these objects most comfortably fit into the clothing they had on then. I noticed that while most men’s pockets could fit in multiple objects, most women’s clothing either did not have pockets, and if they did, could fit in the smaller objects only. On the next screen are images from a small sample of this experiment.

I thought I’d dig a bit deeper, so I logged onto two popular online shopping portals (Ajio and Myntra) and looked through the top 50 results for men’s trousers, women’s trousers, women’s dresses, and women’s skirts. I noticed that clothing such as trousers which are sold for both men and women are more likely to have pockets than clothing styles specific to women, such as dresses and skirts. On the last screen are graphs showing the gender distribution noticed in these results.

Sizable pockets are necessities in everyday clothing, especially with the rapidly changing pace of mobile technologies and with it, increasing phone sizes. As Chelsea G. Summers argues, pockets are private, secret spaces we carry into the public - the less we can carry in them, the lesser freedom we have to navigate these public spaces. The great clothing gender divide in pockets (or the absence of them) is hence, not only sexist, but also political.
